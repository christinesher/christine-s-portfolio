Instagram Reels Acceptance Study

Why users adopt, resist, or avoid new social media features

Overview

This research explores why users accept or reject new features on social media, focusing on Instagram Reels as a case study.

Topic: User acceptance testing for social media features

Core question: How does the Instagram Reels feature influence whether users adopt, resist, or avoid it?

I analyzed two factors:

  • Product factors: design, algorithm, interface, placement
  • Social factors: peer influence, sharing habits, platform culture, identity

A key personal trigger for this study was when Instagram rolled out Reels and my app glitched — the Reels button disappeared. I felt frustrated, left out, and actively searched for fixes. This highlighted how feature adoption is influenced not only by usability, but by social expectations and FOMO.

Method

I conducted semi-structured interviews with 4 participants (18–25), representing different Reels engagement levels:

Segment Participant Age Usage Pattern
Frequent Reels user Dorothy 21 Watches Reels daily; often pulled into scrolling
Light / aesthetic-focused user Erika 21 <5 min per session; prefers TikTok
Reels-neutral / partial avoider Mia 18 Mainly uses IG for messaging; sees Reels as repetitive
Balanced IG + TikTok user CC 25 Uses both apps equally; sees Reels & TikTok as serving different needs

Interview topics included: discovery of Reels, emotional reactions, algorithm trust, social dynamics, usability, and comparisons with TikTok & YouTube Shorts.

Key Insights

1. Reels feels heavily "pushed" into the app

Reels appear everywhere — Feed, Explore, and Reels tab — making it feel unavoidable.

Users felt the app "funnels" you into Reels once you tap one.

Some preferred Reels to stay only in Explore or the Reels tab.

What this means: Instagram is maximizing exposure, but at the cost of user autonomy.

2. TikTok wins on authenticity; Reels wins on convenience

Across all participants:

  • TikTok = more variety, realism, humor, comment culture
  • Reels = aesthetic, random-but-entertaining, lighter version of TikTok

CC's perspective strengthened this insight:

"TikTok has more creator-driven videos, while Instagram gives me random, entertaining short clips — I need both for different moods."

Reels = convenience; TikTok = culture.

3. Reels strengthens relationships through easy sharing

Even users who prefer TikTok still use Reels for social interaction.

Instagram is where their existing contacts already are.

Sharing Reels feels frictionless and socially natural.

CC and Dorothy both said Reels helps them start conversations with friends.

But social visibility can also create anxiety:

Mia and CC dislike that Instagram shows which Reels someone has liked, making them less likely to "like freely."

4. Users fall into clear acceptance profiles

Embedded Scroller (Dorothy)
Uses Reels often; finds it addicting but wouldn't miss it if removed.

Aesthetic Dipper (Erika)
Checks Reels briefly; prefers TikTok's realism and comments.

Functional Communicator (Mia)
Uses Instagram mainly for messaging; sees Reels as repetitive and unnecessary.

Balanced Dual-User (CC)
Uses Instagram and TikTok equally, depending on mood and content type. Would want Reels back if it disappeared because it improves the overall Instagram experience and provides "everything a good social platform should have."

Opportunities

Based on the interviews, users would benefit from:

1. More control over where Reels appear

Allow users to limit Reels in the main feed and keep them separate if preferred.

2. Better algorithm variety & transparency

Reduce repetition and explain why certain videos appear.

3. More privacy around likes

Multiple users want to hide what they like from followers.

4. Leverage Instagram's social strength

Design features around small-group interactions, quick sharing, and conversation threads.
