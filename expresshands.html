ExpressHands

AI-Powered ASL Translation Interface

The Challenge

How might we enable real-time communication between ASL users and non-signers in everyday contexts?

Over 11 million people in the U.S. are deaf or hard of hearing, and American Sign Language (ASL) is their primary mode of communication. Yet most existing tools focus on learning ASL rather than facilitating real-time conversations. This leaves ASL users facing significant barriers in healthcare settings, social interactions, and daily communication with non-signers.

The Problem

Through secondary research and analysis of existing accessibility tools, I identified key pain points:

  • Limited real-time translation tools ‚Äî Most apps focus on ASL learning, not active conversation
  • Loss of emotional context ‚Äî Facial expressions and tone carry critical meaning in ASL that text-based translation loses
  • One-way solutions ‚Äî Existing tools often fail to support bidirectional conversation flow
  • Accessibility barriers in critical contexts ‚Äî Medical, legal, and emergency situations require accurate, immediate communication

Key Design Decisions

Two-way conversation flow

Decision: Both users see real-time translations in their preferred mode (ASL ‚Üí text/speech, speech/text ‚Üí visual signing cues).

Why: True conversation requires bidirectional understanding. Asymmetric tools create power imbalances.

Facial expression & tone detection

Decision: AI interprets facial expressions and body language to convey emotional context, not just literal word translation.

Why: In ASL, a raised eyebrow or head tilt changes meaning entirely ‚Äî this isn't optional information, it's grammatical.

Personalized calibration profiles

Decision: Users answer onboarding questions to train the AI on their individual signing style and expressiveness.

Why: ASL isn't standardized like spoken language ‚Äî regional variations and personal styles exist. Personalization improves accuracy.

Saved conversation history

Decision: Users can review past conversations just like they would in social messaging apps.

Why: This transforms the app from a utility tool into a communication platform. By treating ASL conversations the same way we treat text messages or DMs, it normalizes accessibility and builds ongoing relationships ‚Äî not just one-off translations.

Key User Flows

1. Signing Up

User opens app ‚Üí inputs their personal information or sign-in with other accounts ‚Üí fills out questionaire

2. Translating

Camera activates to begin translation ‚Üí AI recognizes gestures and translates them into text ‚Üí the user can switch translated text languages ‚Üí the non-ASL user can speak to the microphone ‚Üí the ASL user can see the translated text

3. Handling Uncertainty / Low Confidence

AI detects unclear gesture ‚Üí shows "?" indicator + best guess ‚Üí user can tap to correct or re-sign ‚Üí system learns from correction

Why this matters: Transparency about AI confidence prevents miscommunication in critical contexts.

4. Show or Scan QR Code

Goes to profile page to show or scan QR code to connect with others

Why this matters: By connecting with others, the translation process would be more accurate based on the user's expressive style

Accessibility & Ethical Considerations

Error Handling & Edge Cases

  • Poor lighting: Prompts to adjust; text fallback
  • Unclear signing: Shows confidence indicators
  • Network loss: Offline mode; syncs later

Ethical AI Design

  • Bias: Train on diverse signers
  • Privacy: On-device processing
  • Over-reliance: Empower, not replace interpreters

Usability Trade-offs

Why not full auto? User control builds trust.

Why mobile-first? Portability for everyday use.

If I Continued: Validation & Next Steps

What would we test first?

  • ASL accuracy: Test with real users
  • Emotional context: Tone perception tests
  • User trust: Qualitative interviews

Key Metrics

  • Translation accuracy: % correct by context
  • Time to communicate: Speed of understanding
  • Confidence score: User comfort level
  • Correction rate: Manual fix frequency

Riskiest Assumptions

  • AI facial expression accuracy ‚Üí Bias risk
  • Cultural context understanding ‚Üí Miscommunication
  • Mobile-only sufficiency ‚Üí Desktop may be needed
